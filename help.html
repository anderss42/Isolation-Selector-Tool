Isolation Selector Tool Help Guide

This tool assists with the risk assessment process for planning isolations on pipework and equipment, helping determine the minimum isolation category required before carrying out maintenance work.

Prerequisite: Before using this tool, all users must have read and understood TUK-14-C-004 Safe Isolation and Reinstatement of Plant.
# Input Description
1 What is being isolated and why? This is a text entry box that should contain the equipment description and why it is being isolated. Example: P14205-A Oil export pump isolated for mechanical seal change out.
2 Front-end planning checks Two Yes/No questions, shown once a title is entered. If a failure of the isolation could lead to a major accident event, or if it is reasonably practicable to wait for a shutdown, then isolation selection is not appropriate — the tool will advise that the plant should be shut down for this work, and the rest of the form is not required.
3 What is the fluid being isolated? Choose the fluid from the drop-down. Fluids are grouped into four GHS-based Fluid Groups (1 Highly Dangerous, 2 Dangerous, 3 Hazardous, 4 Non-hazardous). The tool infers the group from your selection. If the fluid is not listed, choose Other, type the substance name and select its group manually.
4 Operating temperature Enter the operating temperature of the medium in °C. Temperature can escalate the fluid group — for example a non-hazardous (Group 4) fluid running above 50 °C is treated as Group 3, and above 60 °C (or below −10 °C) it is treated as Group 1. The tool always uses the more onerous of the fluid's base group and the temperature-derived group.
5 How long will containment be broken for? This is not how long the total task will take; it is the length of time the pipework will be left open and exposed should the isolation fail. Choose One shift or less or More than one shift.
6 Yes / No questions Six Yes/No questions are revealed one at a time as each is answered. Answer each in turn:
  • Is the risk from the installation and removal of positive isolation greater than using a valve isolation? — If No, positive isolation (spade or disconnection) will be auto-applied and no isolation type selection is required in Stage 2. If Yes, you will be asked to select the best available valve isolation in Stage 2.
  • Hot work — spark potential or naked flame, in the task or vicinity. Escalates the minimum required category to I for Fluid Groups 1 & 2.
  • Confined space entry (CSE) — forces a Category I (positive isolation) requirement regardless of fluid group or duration.
  • Flare, LP vent or closed drains — where work is against a single valve on an LP flare, vent or closed drains system, a Category III isolation may be acceptable subject to additional controls.
  • Instrument small-bore tubing (SBT) personal isolation — for instrument impulse line isolations; minimum Category IIB for Groups 1 & 2, Category III for Groups 3 & 4.
  • Within a boundary isolation — where a boundary isolation is already in place, a single local valve (Category III) is sufficient.
The ICC number field and the Next button appear once all six questions have been answered.
7 ICC number (optional) If known, enter the ICC number. It is used to name the saved PDF.
8 Next button Once the above is entered press Next to display the line specification section. If anything is missing you will be prompted and cannot continue.
9 Enter a description of the line / isolation point Describe the particular line or point being assessed, e.g. "Suction pipework of export pump". A separate assessment should be carried out for each isolation point — for example one for suction and one for discharge — especially where connected live systems carry different fluids.
10 Highest standard of isolation that can reasonably be applied This section only appears if you answered Yes to the positive isolation risk question in Stage 1. If you answered No, positive isolation (spade or disconnection) is automatically applied and no selection is required here.

Where a selection is required, choose the best valve isolation you can physically apply at this point. The options available are, in descending order of strength:
  • Category IIA — Proven Double Block and Bleed (DBB)
  • Category IIA — Proven Twin Seal Valve
  • Category IIB — Proven Single Block and Bleed (SBB)
  • Category III — Non-Proven Single or Double Valve
Click the icon for the highest standard you can apply. Spade is not directly selectable here as it is handled by the positive isolation risk question.
11 Calculate button Press this once all information is entered. The tool determines the minimum required isolation category and compares it with the isolation you selected.
12 Output screen Shows the information entered, the minimum required isolation category (I, IIA, IIB or III) and the category your selected isolation provides. If your selection meets or exceeds the minimum it confirms the isolation can be used. If it does not, it displays a warning, states that a Level 2 risk assessment / deviation is required, and shows who must authorise the deviation.
13 Isolation categories
  • I — Positive isolation: spade or disconnection.
  • IIA — Proven Double Block and Bleed (DBB): double valve arrangement with body bleed.
  • IIA — Proven Twin Seal Valve: valve with integrated twin seals and body bleed (equivalent strength to DBB).
  • IIB — Proven leak-tight Single Block and Bleed (SBB).
  • III — Non-proven single or double valve (double preferred if available).
14 Return to input data button Takes you back to the input screen where changes can be made.
15 Save as PDF Creates a PDF of the outcome and downloads it to your downloads folder. This can be attached to your isolation within TCOW.
16 Finished When finished with the selector tool simply close the window.
